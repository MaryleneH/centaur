# Reading Guide

Five papers. One paragraph each. You do not need to read the full PDFs to attend the workshop; you do need to know which paper says what, because we cite them by name in the build.

The five are split into two camps. Kosmyna and Peterson are the cognitive replacement camp: what happens when humans hand off thinking to AI. Csaszar / Ketkar / Kim and Caosun / Aral are the augmentation camp: what happens when humans pair with AI on real work. Acemoglu / Kong / Ozdaglar zooms out to the system level. Centaur is the product that takes the augmentation camp seriously without ignoring what the replacement camp measured.

## Kosmyna et al., 2025. Your Brain on ChatGPT: Accumulation of Cognitive Debt when Using an AI Assistant for Essay Writing Task.

MIT Media Lab. EEG study, fifty-four participants, three groups: brain-only, search engine, LLM. The LLM group's brain connectivity was the weakest of the three across multiple frequency bands. They also showed reduced recall of their own essays minutes after writing them. The paper coins "cognitive debt" for the long-run cost of repeatedly outsourcing the work your brain was about to do. This is the empirical floor under the whole workshop. When we say careless AI use makes you duller, this is the study we are pointing at. Surfaces in the Chat block's Triple Diamond pass on Needs and in the Cowork synthesis pass on cognitive replacement findings.

## Caosun and Aral, 2026. The Augmentation Trap: AI Productivity and the Cost of Cognitive Offloading.

MIT Sloan. Dynamic model of a manager deciding how much AI to give a worker over time. Even a rational, fully-informed manager picks an AI intensity that produces steady-state skill loss: the worker ends up less productive than before AI was introduced, because the productivity boost front-loads and the skill erosion compounds. Add short-termism or external skill value and you get the augmentation trap: the worker is worse off than if AI had never been adopted at all. Critically, when AI's productivity benefit depends less on user expertise, inexperienced workers deskill toward zero and experienced workers stay flat: the gap permanently widens. This is the counter-evidence to "augmentation always wins." The augmentation camp has to design for this finding, not dodge it.

## Csaszar, Ketkar, Kim, 2024. Artificial Intelligence and Strategic Decision-Making: Evidence from Entrepreneurs and Investors.

Strategy Science. Empirical work from a startup accelerator showing that current LLMs can generate and evaluate strategies at a level comparable to experienced entrepreneurs and investors. Not "AI beats humans"; "AI plays at the same table." The paper unpacks what changes when LLMs sit inside the search-represent-aggregate loop of strategic thinking. Surfaces in the Chat block's strategic foresight pass and in the Cowork pass on what the product needs to do in the user's actual workflow. The reason we cite it: it kills the lazy framing that AI is only for low-stakes tasks. Strategic work is on the table; the question is how the human stays in the loop without becoming decorative.

## Acemoglu, Kong, Ozdaglar, 2026. AI, Human Cognition and Knowledge Collapse.

NBER Working Paper 34910. Dynamic model where agentic AI substitutes for human effort in producing context-specific decisions. The substitution reduces the flow of new public signals into the community's shared knowledge stock. General knowledge depreciates. Past a critical accuracy threshold for AI and a critical elasticity for human effort, the only stable steady state is complete knowledge collapse: nobody is investing in learning, everybody is consuming AI output, the shared base hollows out. This is the systems-level companion to Kosmyna. It surfaces in the Cowork synthesis pass and in the workshop's close, where we name why an individual cognitive enhancement tool matters beyond the individual.

## Peterson, 2024. AI and the Problem of Knowledge Collapse.

University of Poitiers. arXiv. The shorter, earlier framing of the same problem Acemoglu later formalized. Peterson's simulation shows that even a modest cost discount on AI-generated content (the paper uses 20%) pulls public beliefs 2.3x further from truth than the no-discount baseline, because the cheapness of center-of-distribution AI output crowds out the long-tail human-generated content the truth actually lives in. We cite this paper for the mechanism, in clear language, before Acemoglu's heavier formalism. It surfaces in the Chat block's discovery pass when we frame the user need: the user is not just dulling themselves; they are dulling the corpus.
